### Product & Technology

**Q: Walk me through how a patient experiences this.**
A: Two minutes a day. They open the app, run a structured check-in — mood, sleep, stress, energy, dissociation, anxiety on 0–10 scales, plus medication and symptom logging. They optionally journal in one of three modes. They get back a 2–3 sentence insight grounded in that day's actual numbers. Over time, the trends view shows them their own patterns with linear regression and statistical significance markers — so they're seeing what's actually changing, not what they remember. Before an appointment, they generate a Mode B summary in plain language to bring with them.

**Q: Walk me through what a provider sees.**
A: They open the provider dashboard. They see their full panel — 20 to 30 patients — with each patient's 14-day mood average, trend direction, days since last check-in, urgency tier, and any AI-detected alerts. They can scan their whole panel in under a minute. Before each appointment, they generate a Mode C clinical summary — trajectory, quantitative averages with trend statistics, medication signal, lifestyle data, qualitative themes, symptom patterns, flags, and suggested discussion topics. The summary takes them three minutes to read and replaces what would otherwise be 30 minutes of chart review. *Offer to demo if they ask.*

**Q: What stops the AI from saying something it shouldn't?**
A: Three layers. The system prompt instructs against diagnostic language, medication recommendations, and clinical interpretation. The quantitative scores are computed deterministically in Python before any AI call, so the model never invents a number. And every output is post-processed against a forbidden-language list that catches diagnostic phrasing, medication recommendations, and certainty overclaims at the string level. The crisis detection runs *before* the AI is invoked — if self-harm language is in the input, the model never sees it; the patient gets crisis resources and nothing else.

**Q: What if Claude hallucinates anyway?**
A: Two responses. First, the deterministic scoring and forbidden-language pass are designed to prevent the highest-risk hallucinations — bad numbers and diagnostic claims. Second, the entire product is framed for the clinician as pattern surfacing, not clinical guidance. The provider is the safety net by design. Every Mode C summary explicitly ends with the methodology note that scores are computed in code and output is post-processed — it's not pretending to be more than it is.

**Q: What about Anthropic deprecating the model?**
A: The system prompts are model-agnostic and we test against new model releases as part of standard maintenance. If Anthropic deprecated the API tomorrow, we could move to a different LLM provider in a few weeks — the structured-data architecture is what matters, not which model generates the prose. The deterministic scoring engine and the safety architecture are entirely independent of the model.

**Q: What's your mobile story?**
A: The platform runs in mobile browsers today via the React SPA. Native iOS and Android apps are explicitly funded in the use-of-funds breakdown as part of the $95K engineering allocation — mobile hardening is one of the first dollars deployed because that's where daily check-in compliance lives or dies.

**Q: What about HIPAA, PHI, and security architecture?**
A: We're built on Supabase with row-level security and Postgres encryption at rest. All sub-processors — Supabase, Render, Anthropic — have BAA-eligible plans. The $60K compliance line item in the raise funds the BAA formalization, the initial security audit, the privacy policy and TOS, and the path to a SOC 2 Type 1 attestation. Until those are in place, we run pilots under research-style consent rather than full clinical deployment.

**Q: Is this a medical device?**
A: No, by design. FDA Cures Act Section 3060 created an exemption for clinical decision support software that surfaces information a licensed provider could independently evaluate. CognaSync explicitly does not make recommendations — it surfaces patterns. The system prompt, the deterministic scoring, the forbidden-language pass, and the Mode C output structure are all designed around that exemption. If we later add the decompensation risk forecasting on the roadmap, that's the moment the regulatory question changes — and we'd address it through the proper SaMD pathway at that point.

### Regulatory & Compliance

**Q: Tell me more about Cures Act 3060.**
A: It defines four conditions for the clinical decision support exemption: the software intends to display information about a patient; the information is intended to support a clinical recommendation; the recommendation is independently reviewable by the clinician; and the software identifies the basis of the recommendation. CognaSync threads all four — we surface structured information, the provider makes any clinical recommendation, and the methodology note in every Mode C summary makes the basis of every surfaced pattern explicit. We're not making diagnoses or treatment recommendations; we're aggregating tracked variables and reporting co-occurrences with statistical anchors.

**Q: What's your data privacy architecture?**
A: All patient data lives in Supabase with Postgres-level encryption at rest and TLS in transit. RLS policies enforce per-user data access at the database layer. Patient PHI is never sent to the LLM as identifying data — only structured scores and de-identified content. The journal entries that *do* get sent to the model are protected under the BAA we'll formalize with Anthropic as part of the compliance spend. Patient data export is built in so patients can take their data with them at any time.

**Q: What about state-level regulations?**
A: Behavioral health is heavily state-regulated. Initial deployment is Arizona only, where I'm based, which keeps the regulatory surface area manageable. Multi-state expansion will follow the standard playbook — state-by-state telehealth and behavioral health licensing review, working with healthcare counsel. The Tier 1 individual-clinician model also limits state complexity since the clinician owns the licensure relationship.

These are the questions designed to test you, not to extract information. Answer them flatly and confidently. Long answers signal anxiety.

**Q: What's the bear case for CognaSync?**
A: Three real risks. One, providers don't adopt — clinical workflows are sticky and adding any new tool faces friction even when the tool is good. Two, behavioral data isn't actionable enough — the patterns we surface are interesting but don't change clinical decisions, in which case the product becomes a nice-to-have rather than essential. Three, big tech or a well-capitalized incumbent decides this is a strategic priority and out-resources us before we have provider network density. I take all three seriously. The pilot in Q1 is designed to test the first two early. The third we manage by moving fast and building real provider relationships.

**Q: Why hasn't a clinician built this?**
A: A handful are trying. The reason most haven't is that clinicians who become founders typically build EHR-adjacent products because that's the workflow they know. CognaSync requires a different design instinct — pattern surfacing without crossing into clinical recommendation. That sits between the clinician mindset and the technologist mindset. The founder who built it had to live the failure case to see the gap clearly.

**Q: What's the most likely failure mode?**
A: Slow provider adoption combined with insufficient capital to extend the runway long enough to overcome it. The mitigation is being honest about what milestones I need to hit and at what pace, and being willing to course-correct on go-to-market if Q1 reveals the original assumptions were wrong.

**Q: Why should I invest in this instead of the next AI mental health pitch?**
A: I won't make that argument. You should invest in this if the product, the clinical seriousness, and the founder convince you it's real. Most AI mental health pitches are clinician-light wellness apps with chatbots. This is a behavioral intelligence layer with a deterministic scoring engine, a programmatic safety architecture, and a founder who built it from inside the problem. If that's not the signal that matters to you, this probably isn't the right fit.

**Q: Has anyone said no?**
A: *Honest.* "No structured pitches have been declined because the round is just opening. The booklet has been seen by [people you've shown it to]. I'm in this meeting because the right early conversations matter more than volume."

**Q: Convince me this isn't going to be the Theranos of mental health.**
A: The comparison would be apt if I were making medical claims I couldn't substantiate. CognaSync doesn't make medical claims. It surfaces patterns from patient-reported data, anchored to specific N's and statistical thresholds, with explicit methodology disclosure in every output. The architecture is designed to be auditable. The forbidden-language pass exists specifically to prevent the kind of overclaim that would put a mental health product in Theranos territory. I'm aware of the historical pattern. The design protects against it.

**Q: What if you and William have a falling out?**
A: Founder agreements with vesting and dispute-resolution clauses are part of the legal work in the post-raise compliance budget. The honest answer is that William and I have known each other long enough to know how we operate. If something genuinely went wrong, the equity structure protects the company, and the operational impact on the product would be minimal because I'm doing the building.

## What you should NOT volunteer

A short list of things to keep off the table unless asked directly.

- **Specific competitor weaknesses.** Don't bash Blueprint or Osmind. Both are well-funded and respected. Acknowledge their work and position around what they don't do.
- **Personal details beyond what the booklet shares.** The crisis story is in the booklet. Do not add new vulnerabilities tomorrow. If asked deeper, answer briefly and redirect.
- **Other investor conversations.** If asked, be honest. Do not name-drop or oversell interest you don't have.
- **Detailed clinical opinions.** You are not the clinician. When asked clinical questions, route to your clinical advisor plan. "That's exactly the kind of question my clinical advisors are being brought on to answer formally."
- **Negative things about education or your prior career.** Stay neutral. The story is forward-looking.

## How to close the meeting

The last five minutes shape the follow-up more than the first five. When the energy starts to wind down:

1. **Restate the ask, named.** *"John, the most useful thing for me from this conversation would be an introduction to [specific person] at Valleywise's behavioral health program for an exploratory pilot conversation. Jonathan, the most useful thing from your side would be a follow-on conversation about whether Idealab Arizona's resources could engage with a built, non-ASU-affiliated company in your medical/AI vertical."*

2. **Ask for the next step.** *"What would you want to see from me in the next two weeks to keep this moving?"* This forces specificity and gives you the rubric for your follow-up.

3. **Acknowledge the family connection one more time, lightly.** *"I appreciate you both taking this seriously rather than treating it as a family conversation. That's exactly what I needed."*

4. **Thank them for their time, get out cleanly.** Do not linger.

**Within 24 hours after the meeting:**

- Separate email to John — reference one specific thing he said, propose one concrete next step.
- Separate email to Jonathan — reference one specific thing he said, propose one concrete next step.
- Note to William — what happened, what you took away, what you're asking for from him in the follow-up.
- Note to whoever set this up (the warm introducer) — what happened, what you appreciated, what you're proposing as next moves.
