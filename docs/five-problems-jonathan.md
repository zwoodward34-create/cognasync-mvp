## Framing

Each of the five problems below was selected against a single test: does solving it improve the patient's life *and* increase revenue or reduce cost for at least one institutional buyer (pharma, payer, or value-based care organization). If a problem only helps the patient, it is not on this list — it is the wrong customer for a company at our stage. If a problem only helps the buyer, it is also not on this list — the patient must benefit or the product is unethical and unsellable.

These five passed both tests.

The product mechanism in every case leverages CognaSync's existing architecture: deterministic scoring computed in code prior to AI generation, four non-negotiable safety rules embedded in every output, forbidden-language sanitization at the post-processing layer, methodology footers on every clinician-facing brief. The pivot is from a patient-self-report model to a passive-data-ingestion model that produces the same provider-facing briefs from substantially less patient friction. The clinical safety architecture survives the pivot intact. The mechanism for collecting data is what changes.

## Problem 1 — Psychiatrists make medication decisions on incomplete, recall-based information

Outpatient psychiatry runs on memory. The patient walks into a thirty-minute visit and tries to summarize what the last two to four weeks of their life looked like. The clinician makes prescribing decisions on what the patient happens to say in the first ten minutes of that conversation. Sleep is asked about as "how have you been sleeping?" — not as a number. Mood is asked about as "how have you been feeling?" — not as a trajectory. Adherence is assumed unless the patient volunteers otherwise. The single most consequential clinical decision in the encounter — whether to continue, escalate, switch, or augment a psychotropic — is made on data that would be considered laughable in any other branch of medicine.

The cost of this is enormous and almost entirely hidden. Patients spend an average of three to five medication trials before finding a regimen that works for depression. Each failed trial is six to twelve weeks of suboptimal care, during which the patient suffers, the payer continues to pay for visits and prescriptions that aren't working, the pharma company collects revenue on a drug that isn't producing the outcome it was prescribed to produce, and the provider's confidence in the next decision is no better than the last one because the feedback loop is dominated by self-report. The Mental Health Million Project estimates the global productivity loss from inadequately treated mental illness at over four trillion dollars annually. A meaningful portion of that loss is attributable to the trial-and-error structure of psychiatric prescribing, which exists because the data needed to do better is not collected.

CognaSync's contribution is a pre-visit brief, generated automatically before every appointment, that gives the clinician the trajectory of mood, sleep, stress, energy, medication adherence, and behavioral signals over the preceding two to twelve weeks. The brief is built from passively collected data — wearables, phone metadata, calendar, pharmacy refill data, and consented AI-conversation extraction — not from patient self-report. The patient does not maintain a daily habit. The provider receives a document that tells them more about the patient's interval than the visit itself will surface. The four non-negotiable safety rules ensure the brief describes patterns, not diagnoses. The methodology footer ensures the clinician can audit any claim against its data anchor.

The buyer for this is the value-based care organization (Headway, Talkiatry, LifeStance, Brave Health, Quartet) or the integrated payer-provider system. They are at risk for outcomes, they have budget for tooling that demonstrably improves them, and the sales cycle is measured in months rather than years. The provider gets a workflow tool that saves them documentation time and improves their clinical decisions. The patient gets better, faster care without doing daily logging work. The payer gets fewer failed trials and shorter time-to-stable-regimen. The pharma company gets patients who reach effective dose and stay on their medication.

## Problem 2 — Half of psychiatric patients abandon their medication within six months, often the right medication taken under the wrong conditions

The persistence problem is the single most expensive failure mode in mental health pharmacotherapy and the one with the most aligned incentives across all four parties. Roughly fifty percent of psychiatric patients discontinue their medication within six months of initiation. The reasons cluster into a small number of categories: side effects the patient never articulated to their provider, dosing schedules incompatible with the patient's actual life, an initial impression that the medication "isn't working" formed at week three when the medication's effect curve hasn't peaked, and confusion about what the medication is supposed to do. In a substantial fraction of cases, the patient was on the right medication and would have stabilized if they had stayed on it under slightly different conditions.

Pharma spends hundreds of millions of dollars annually on patient support programs and adherence interventions for psychiatric drugs. The vast majority of these programs are reminder-based — SMS, email, phone calls from a hub agent — and they are not effective at the persistence problem because they treat adherence as a memory problem rather than as a side-effect, timing, and expectation-setting problem. Payers absorb the downstream cost of discontinuation, which is some combination of relapse, decompensation, emergency department utilization, and hospitalization. The avoidable hospitalizations alone cost the U.S. health system tens of billions of dollars per year.

CognaSync's contribution is passive detection of the conditions that precede discontinuation, before discontinuation happens. Sleep degradation, side-effect symptom patterns (the existing symptom correlation engine), dosing-timing variability, energy collapse, and AI-conversation extraction of side-effect language the patient mentioned to ChatGPT but never to their doctor. When the pattern crosses threshold, the provider is alerted between visits. The intervention is not a reminder — it is a clinical adjustment opportunity in the window before the patient quietly stops taking the medication. The product's role is to make discontinuation visible to the clinician before the patient makes the decision.

The buyer for this is pharma, specifically the medical affairs and patient services teams responsible for the patient hub for a given psychiatric drug. CognaSync becomes the digital layer inside a manufacturer-funded patient support program. The patient receives the product free as part of their prescription benefit. The provider receives the early warning. The pharma company protects the revenue tied to persistence on a drug that is genuinely working. The payer avoids the downstream cost of decompensation. The patient stays on a medication that would have stabilized them if they had not abandoned it for reasons their provider never knew about.

## Problem 3 — Decompensation is detected too late, usually in the emergency department

The highest-cost failure mode in mental health is the one where a patient deteriorates between visits and presents in crisis. A psychiatric hospitalization in the United States ranges from twenty to fifty thousand dollars. An emergency department visit for a psychiatric chief complaint averages between three and seven thousand. The patient experiences the worst outcome the system produces — involuntary holds, restraint, loss of agency, loss of employment, loss of housing in extreme cases, and the trauma of the experience itself. The provider learns about the deterioration in the discharge summary, weeks after the fact. The pharma company loses the patient entirely because hospitalized patients frequently switch regimens during admission and many do not return to outpatient care for months. Decompensation is the negative-sum event around which the entire mental health system is structured to avoid, and the system has almost no instrumentation to predict it.

The clinical signs of early decompensation are well-described in the literature and are largely behavioral: sleep architecture collapse (in both directions — insomnia in depression and anxiety, dramatic reduction in total sleep in mania), nocturnal activity bursts, social app collapse to a single contact or no contacts, message-volume changes, voice-prosody changes in conversations, location-pattern disruption indicating loss of routine, and language patterns in AI-conversation logs that escalate over the days before crisis. None of these signals require the patient to do anything they were not already doing. All of them are accessible with consent. None of them are systematically collected today.

CognaSync's contribution is the integration of these signals into a single risk surface the provider can act on. The deterministic scoring engine already exists for the core variables. The Crash Risk composite (sleep disruption plus nervous system load plus nutrition stability) is already defined. The crisis interception layer already handles the most extreme signal. What the pivot adds is the ingestion layer — the wearables, the phone metadata, the consented AI-conversation extraction — that produces the inputs to the existing engine without requiring the patient to log them by hand. The output is a tiered alert system: informational at low risk, watch at moderate, urgent at high, with the alert routed to the provider's dashboard and not to the patient, because telling a patient they are about to decompensate is itself a destabilizing event.

The buyer is the payer or the value-based care organization. Decompensation prevention is the single largest dollar-value problem in their book. The product needs to demonstrate, in a pilot, that flagged patients had measurably lower utilization than unflagged patients with similar baseline risk. That study is achievable in a population of a few hundred patients over six to twelve months. Once it exists, the contract is six and seven figures per year. The pharma company benefits because patients who avoid hospitalization stay on their existing regimen. The patient benefits in the most direct way the system permits — they avoid the worst outcome it produces.

## Problem 4 — Patients cannot articulate what is actually happening to them between visits

Patients know something is happening. They cannot translate it into the language clinicians use. A patient experiencing the early phase of an SSRI activation event does not know to say "I have racing thoughts, my sleep has dropped from seven hours to four, and I have started a project I cannot finish." They say "I feel weird." A patient experiencing the cognitive slowing that some antipsychotics produce does not know to say "my information processing speed is degraded." They say "I feel slow." The clinician receives the vague version, asks one or two clarifying questions in the time they have, and writes the note based on the impression. The actual signal — racing thoughts, sleep collapse, project-initiation burst — is sitting in the patient's data and never makes it into the clinical record.

This is not a patient failure. It is an articulation problem. Asking someone to describe their internal state in clinical vocabulary in real time, under the time pressure of an outpatient visit, with a stranger asking the questions, is asking for a translation that humans are not well-equipped to do. The patient logs of CognaSync's current product partially solve this — they create a record the patient can refer to — but they require daily work the patient does not want to do and frequently abandons after the first few weeks.

CognaSync's contribution under the pivot is to do the translation for them. The patient's behavioral data is collected passively. The brief sent to the provider before the visit translates the data into clinical vocabulary: "Sleep averaged 4.8 hours over the past nine days, down from a 7.1-hour baseline. Late-night phone activity increased by 240% over the same window. Three journal entries used activation-pattern language. Workload friction self-reports trended upward." The patient is not asked to do the translation. The data does it for them. The provider walks into the visit knowing what to ask about.

The buyer is the same buyer as Problem 1 — the value-based care organization or integrated provider system that wants its clinicians to be more effective per encounter. The patient benefits because they are seen and understood by their clinician with a precision that is otherwise impossible in a thirty-minute slot. The provider benefits because they walk into the visit with the high-signal information already in hand. The pharma company benefits because medication response and side effects are surfaced accurately rather than through the lossy channel of patient self-report. The payer benefits because the encounter produces a clinical decision based on real data rather than recalled impression.

## Problem 5 — Medication side effects and interactions present as patterns the patient cannot connect to the medication

Two weeks after an SSRI initiation, the patient develops headaches. They do not connect the headaches to the medication. They mention them to a friend, to their AI assistant, to themselves in a journal — but they do not connect them. By the time the next appointment arrives, the headaches are a separate complaint, presented as a separate problem, and the SSRI continues. Six weeks later the patient has discontinued the medication, telling the provider it "wasn't working," when in fact it was working and the side effect they could not contextualize is the reason it was abandoned.

This pattern repeats across the entire psychopharmacology landscape. Stimulant medications produce sleep degradation the patient blames on stress. Mood stabilizers produce tremor the patient blames on caffeine. Antipsychotics produce metabolic shifts the patient blames on diet. The brain does not naturally connect a change at week zero to a symptom shift fourteen days later. Side effects and interactions are detected when they are severe enough to be undeniable, which is too late — by then the medication has often been abandoned, or the side effect has produced its own clinical event, or the interaction has cascaded.

CognaSync's contribution here is a feature that already exists in the product. The symptom-correlation engine — `find_symptom_correlations()` in the existing code — detects when a logged symptom co-occurs with other behavioral variables at a delta exceeding 1.5 standard deviations over a fourteen-day window, and surfaces the medication-context bridge when a medication event preceded the symptom onset by less than fourteen days. The provider sees: "Headaches reported on seven of twenty-two days. Sleep disruption elevated on symptom days. Escitalopram initiation logged five days before first symptom entry." The patient sees, in their Mode B brief, a gentle framing that the pattern is worth bringing up at the appointment. The clinician makes the call. The AI describes the co-occurrence. The clinical interpretation is the human's job.

Under the passive-data pivot, the inputs to this engine come from the wearable and the phone — sleep architecture, activity, voice prosody — rather than from patient logging. The same engine produces the same outputs with less patient friction. The buyer for this layer is pharma, specifically the safety and pharmacovigilance teams. Real-world side-effect detection is a regulatory requirement that pharma satisfies poorly today through MedWatch and clinician-initiated reports. A consented, passive, real-world surveillance layer for adverse drug reactions in psychiatric drugs is a product pharma will pay for, both for the post-market evidence and for the early intervention opportunity it creates. The patient benefits because side effects are detected before they cause the patient to abandon a working medication. The provider benefits because the medication decision is data-anchored. The payer benefits because adverse drug reaction-driven utilization is reduced.

## Synthesis — Which to attack first, and why

The five problems are real, but they are not equally addressable on the same timeline, with the same buyer, or by the same product surface. The order matters.

The first attack is **Problem 1 — the pre-visit brief for value-based care organizations.** This is the shortest sales cycle (three to six months), the most direct product-buyer match (the brief is the product, the VBC organization is the buyer, the provider is the user), and the most defensible early proof point because the ROI argument is operational rather than clinical (documentation time saved per encounter, clinical decisions made on better data). A signed pilot with one of the four named VBC organizations within ninety days is the goal. The brief is built from a smaller, simpler passive-data set (wearable plus pharmacy refill plus calendar) and does not require the more speculative ingestion layers.

The second attack is **Problem 2 — medication persistence, sold to pharma.** This requires the early proof point from Problem 1 to be in place because pharma will want clinical-grade evidence that the product affects persistence before they commit budget. The sales cycle is eighteen to twenty-four months but the contracts are seven and eight figures. The right entry point is a top-twenty pharma's medical affairs team with a psychiatric drug coming off patent in the next five years and an interest in defending persistence on the existing molecule.

**Problem 3 — decompensation prevention, sold to payers** — is the largest dollar-value opportunity in the list but the longest cycle and the most evidence-heavy. It requires a real outcomes study to make the argument, and the study itself is the product. A reasonable horizon is a regional Blues plan or Medicaid managed care pilot in year two, with measurable outcomes by year three.

**Problems 4 and 5 are not separate go-to-market motions.** They are features inside the products built for Problems 1, 2, and 3. The articulation problem (Problem 4) is solved by the same passive-data brief that solves the provider data problem (Problem 1). The medication side-effect detection problem (Problem 5) is solved by the same correlation engine that detects the persistence-risk signals (Problem 2). The five problems are not five products. They are three products that solve five problems, with the safety architecture serving as the connective tissue across all of them.

The pivot's economic spine, then, is: a single passive-data ingestion and analysis platform that produces three distinct clinician-facing surfaces (pre-visit brief, persistence-risk alert, decompensation-risk alert), sold to three distinct buyers (value-based care organizations, pharma, payers), with the patient receiving the product free in every case because the patient is the data source, not the customer. The four-way alignment Jonathan asked about is not aspirational — it is the structural property that makes the model fundable, scalable, and defensible.
