# Meeting Prep — John Graham (Sunbelt Holdings) + Jonathan Cohen (Idealab Arizona)

**Subject:** CognaSync partnership / investor exploration
**Prepared:** May 17, 2026

---

## 1. The Real Game Being Played

These two are not random and they are not independent. **John Graham is a Founding Investor of Idealab Arizona.** Jonathan Cohen is the co-founder and Chief Innovation Officer. You are walking into a structured ecosystem moment with two people whose decisions move in lockstep — the civic patriarch who can vouch for you across the Arizona healthcare and academic networks, and the operator who decides whether Idealab's studio resources get pointed at you.

That changes how you should approach the room. This is not two meetings stitched together. It is one meeting in which two complementary forms of leverage are sitting across from you. Treat them as a unit while still asking each the questions only they can answer.

The center of gravity of this meeting is not your pitch. It is whether they walk away believing CognaSync is a serious, clinically defensible product run by a serious operator who can be trusted with healthcare system introductions. Graham's network into Valleywise Health Foundation and other Arizona health systems is the most valuable asset on the table — larger than any check size. If he leaves the meeting thinking *"I'd put my name behind introducing him to the right people at Valleywise,"* you've won, regardless of what happens with capital.

## 1A. What the Warm Introduction Changes

This was set up by a warm introduction from someone Graham and Cohen both trust. That changes a few things meaningfully — not the substance of what you're walking in with, but the dynamics around it.

**You inherit credibility from the introducer.** They're predisposed to see something. The "earn the next 30 minutes" framing softens — you don't need to prove you're worth the meeting in the first five minutes, because the introducer already vouched for that. Use the saved energy to go deeper on substance instead.

**The bar on the floor is: don't burn the introducer's social capital.** This is the most important consequence of a warm intro. How you show up reflects on the person who put you in the room. Senior people remember who introduced them to someone serious vs. someone who wasted their time, and they update on the introducer accordingly. Treat that responsibility as a forcing function — it's a useful one.

**Find out what the introducer told them about you, before the meeting if at all possible.** Call or text the introducer. Ask: *"What did you say to John and Jonathan about CognaSync when you set this up? I want to make sure I'm walking in aligned with the framing you gave them."* This single phone call is one of the highest-leverage things you can do this week. It tells you what hooks are already set in the room, what assumptions they're bringing in, and where you need to either confirm or gently reframe.

**Acknowledge the introducer at the top of the meeting, briefly.** Something like: *"Thanks for making the time — [Introducer] spoke highly of both of you and the work you're doing, which is why I wanted to make sure I came prepared with substance rather than just a pitch."* This signals you understand the relational architecture, honors the introducer, and sets a serious tone in one sentence.

**They will almost certainly talk to the introducer after the meeting.** Plan for it. The introducer's downstream impression of how the meeting went will partly come from Graham and Cohen, and partly from you. Which leads to the follow-up rule below.

**Loop the introducer into your follow-up.** Within 24 hours after the meeting, send the introducer a short, sincere note: what you took away, what you appreciated about each of them, what next step you're proposing. This does two things — it closes the loop respectfully, and it gives the introducer a coherent story to tell if Graham or Cohen check in with them. Founders consistently forget this step and consistently lose the next introduction.

## 2. The Structural Question You Need to Resolve Before You Walk In

Idealab Arizona's stated thesis, in their own words, is **co-founding ASU-affiliated tech startups in climate, medical, AI, and advanced manufacturing**, taking them from idea to product-market fit. CognaSync is already built. You are not an ASU spin-out. That is a model mismatch you need to be ready to navigate without it lurking unaddressed.

You have three plausible structural framings, and you should be fluent in all of them so the conversation can settle where it naturally fits:

**Framing A — Idealab AZ engages with CognaSync through a non-standard structure.** Maybe Graham invests personally (he's been angel investing since 2018), Idealab AZ provides studio resources via an advisory or partner-equity model, and the typical co-founding equity structure flexes. This is the highest-upside outcome if they want to lean in.

**Framing B — Idealab AZ becomes a channel, not a holder of equity.** They open ASU clinical research relationships, GPEC market-entry support, and intro-level network access. You raise capital separately — possibly from Graham personally or from his network.

**Framing C — The meeting converts into introductions, not a direct relationship.** Graham connects you to Valleywise Health Foundation's behavioral health leadership, the ASU biomedical commercialization arm, and any healthcare-focused AZ angels in his network. Idealab AZ remains an interested but uninvolved party.

You don't need to pick one in advance. You need to be ready to *recognize* which one is forming as the conversation unfolds, and to actively steer it toward the framing that fits.

## 4. How to Approach the Meeting

**Lead with the clinical safety architecture, not the tech stack.** The four non-negotiable safety rules in your spec — never diagnose, never advise medication changes, describe data not clinical meaning, route in crisis — are the most differentiating thing you've built. Most behavioral health AI is sloppy with these lines. You aren't. That's the moat in any clinical conversation.

The opening framing should position CognaSync as a **pattern-recognition layer that explicitly does not cross clinical lines**, designed to make provider visits better, not replace them. That framing earns respect from anyone who has spent time around clinicians. Graham has, through Valleywise.

**Be the operator, not the founder pitching.** They've been pitched by founders. They have not been pitched by someone who has thought as deeply about clinical safety boundaries as your specification demonstrates. The tone should be: serious person, building a serious product, here to figure out whether there's a real fit. Not: here to convince you to like me.

**Resist the urge to fill silence.** These are senior people. They are evaluating you constantly — what you choose to say, what you choose not to say, how you handle pushback, whether you can sit with a pause. Answer well, then stop. Let them ask.

**Be the most prepared person in the room.** Know who else is on Graham's boards, know Idealab AZ's four focus areas cold, know what's been happening in behavioral health funding in 2026 (it's a renaissance year — Talkiatry $210M, Grow Therapy $150M Series D, the niche/underserved-population thesis is what's getting funded). You don't need to bring any of this up. You need them to feel, in the texture of the conversation, that you've done the work.

## 5. Preparation Homework

Run through this list before the meeting. Anything not done is a risk.

**On them and the ecosystem:**
- Read both Idealab Arizona launch announcements (Oct 2024) and the SOM partnership announcement (April 2025). Note the structure of those deals.
- Skim Graham's recent public quotes and any Valley Leadership / Greater Phoenix Leadership speeches. Pick up his vocabulary.
- Know the names: Allen Morgan, Bill Gross, the GPEC leadership, current Valleywise CEO and behavioral health director.
- Know the 2026 behavioral health funding landscape — the shift from broad consumer mental health apps to niche / clinical / outcomes-focused plays. CognaSync sits firmly in the niche/clinical bucket. Frame it that way.

**On your numbers:**
- Burn rate, runway, monthly operating cost, realistic 12-month build/sales plan. They will ask.
- A defensible early-stage pricing model (provider seats, health system contracts, eventual payer relationships).
- A specific number if asked "how much are you raising and on what terms" — even if the answer is "we're not formally raising, but we'd structure something for the right partner at roughly $X on $Y."

**On the regulatory question (this one matters):**
- Know FDA's *Clinical Decision Support Software* guidance and where CognaSync sits relative to it. Pattern-surfacing tools that explicitly do not make clinical recommendations sit in a different regulatory category than diagnostic AI. Your spec was built around this distinction. Be ready to articulate it cleanly in 60 seconds.
- Know the SaMD (Software as a Medical Device) framework at the conceptual level.
- This single question will sort founders into "has thought about it" vs. "hasn't." Don't get caught in the wrong bucket.

**On the team / single-founder question:**
- If you're solo, own it. Don't apologize. Have a clear answer for "what does the team look like in 12 months and what's the first hire."

## 6. What to Bring

A **one-page executive summary**. Not a deck. A deck invites skimming. A one-pager forces them to read the substance. On that page: what CognaSync is in two sentences, the clinical safety architecture in three sentences, the stage you're at, the 12-month plan, the specific ask from this conversation.

A **printed two-page redacted sample of a Mode C provider summary** — actual output the system would produce for a hypothetical patient. This is the proof. Anyone can claim to have built behavioral health AI. Showing them what the AI actually produces — anchored to data, citing N's, no clinical overreach — is what makes the safety thinking tangible. Provider quality of output is the strongest demo you have.

A **printed one-pager of the four non-negotiable safety rules plus the symptom pattern detection logic**. This is the kind of artifact a clinician finds unusually thoughtful. Graham sits on Valleywise. He will recognize the difference between this and the typical AI pitch.

A **laptop ready to demo** the patient and provider interfaces — but do not lead with it. Wait until they ask. If they don't ask, offer it as the meeting closes: *"If you have ten more minutes, I'd love to show you what a provider sees when they pull up a patient summary."*

A **short, written list of 3–5 AZ healthcare or behavioral health contacts** you would most want Graham to consider introducing you to. Specific names, not vague categories. Bring it. Don't hand it to him in the meeting — reference it only if introductions come up. The point of having it is to show, if asked, that you've thought about exactly where his network would be most useful.

## 7. Questions to Ask Them

**For Graham — the questions are about network and signal:**

The single most important question you can ask him is:

> *"You've been deeply involved with Valleywise's behavioral health programs. What do their providers most struggle with that a behavioral pattern-tracking layer could realistically help?"*

This question signals respect for his network, surfaces concrete clinical needs from someone close to them, and tests whether he's willing to think operationally with you. His answer will tell you whether the meeting is going to convert into something real.

Follow-ups:
> *"Among the organizations you're connected to, who's actively looking at digital tools to support outpatient behavioral health care?"*

> *"What's your read on the Arizona behavioral health landscape over the next three to five years — consolidating, expanding, where's the capital flowing?"*

**For Cohen — the questions are about fit and structure:**

The single most important question is the structural one, asked directly:

> *"Idealab Arizona's stated focus is co-founding ASU-affiliated startups. How do you think about companies like CognaSync — already built, not ASU-affiliated, but in your target vertical? Have you done anything like that yet, and what would the structure look like if you did?"*

Surface the model mismatch yourself. Better to put it on the table than have it lurk through the whole meeting.

Follow-ups:
> *"What does an ideal portfolio company at Idealab AZ look like 18 months in?"*

> *"Where is the studio strongest — go-to-market support, technical resources, clinical and regulatory relationships, capital, network? What's the highest-leverage thing CognaSync could draw on?"*

**For both, at the end:**

> *"If you decided to be helpful to CognaSync in the next 90 days, what would the most useful form of that look like to you?"*

This question disarms vague offers and forces specificity. People often say yes to general help and then never follow through. Specifics get acted on.

## 8. What They'll Likely Ask You — Be Ready

- **"What's your background?"** — 60-second answer. They want to know whether you can ship.
- **"Who's on the team?"** — If solo, own it; describe the first hire and the 12-month team plan.
- **"Have you talked to providers?"** — Have specific names ready. If you haven't talked to clinicians yet, say so honestly and ask for introductions.
- **"How does this make money?"** — Provider seats, health system contracts, eventually payer contracts. Have a defensible early-stage pricing model.
- **"What about HIPAA? PHI? Security?"** — Know your architecture answers cold. Supabase RLS, encryption, data residency, the BAA story when you're ready to sign one.
- **"What about regulation — is this a medical device?"** — Answer the SaMD / Clinical Decision Support Software question crisply. This is the single highest-stakes technical question you'll be asked.
- **"What's your moat?"** — The clinical safety architecture is the moat, plus the behavioral data network effect as multiple providers come on.
- **"How much are you raising and on what terms?"** — Have an answer. "We're not raising yet" is fine if true. "I don't know yet" reads as unserious.
- **"Why hasn't someone else built this?"** — Honest answer: most teams either ship clinically irresponsible AI features fast or build clinically responsible products slowly. The spec you've written demonstrates you can do both. Say that.

## 9. Decisions You Need to Make Before the Meeting

These are choices only you can make. Lock them in before you walk in. Walking in without an answer to any of these is a self-inflicted wound.

1. **What is your single most important ask from this meeting?** Pick one. Not three. The clearest candidate is: *a Graham introduction to Valleywise behavioral health leadership for a pilot conversation.* If that's right, build the meeting around earning it.

2. **What is the smallest concrete next step that would constitute a successful outcome?** "We agree to meet again in two weeks with X person from your network" is concrete. "They seem interested" is not.

3. **Are you raising capital right now or not?** If yes — what amount, what terms, what use of funds. If no — what would make you start, and is this meeting that moment?

4. **What is the meeting format?** 30-minute coffee or 60-minute structured conversation? If you don't know, ask the assistant who set it up. The materials you bring should match the format.

5. **What is your one-sentence positioning?** Try this one on for size:
   > *"CognaSync is a behavioral pattern-tracking layer for outpatient mental health — it surfaces what's happening in a patient's data so providers can have more informed conversations, without ever crossing into diagnosis or medication recommendations."*

   If that sentence is wrong, rewrite it before the meeting. But have *one* sentence you can lead with.

## 10. The Post-Meeting Play

Within 24 hours, send each of them a **separate** email — not the same email. Each should reference a specific thing they said in the meeting and propose one concrete next step.

To Graham: a specific introduction request, or a follow-up coffee to discuss whatever was the strongest thread.

To Cohen: a follow-on conversation about structural fit, or a specific Idealab resource you'd want to explore.

A thank-you that says nothing is worse than no email. Make every follow-up move the relationship forward.

If they offered to connect you with anyone in the meeting, name the person in your follow-up and ask explicitly for the introduction. Vague offers die quickly. Named asks get acted on.

**Don't forget the introducer.** Send a separate short note within 24 hours. Tell them what landed, what you appreciated, and what you're proposing as a next step with Graham and Cohen. The introducer is going to hear about this meeting from the other side regardless — you want them to have your version too, and you want them to know you closed the loop with respect for the favor they did you.

## 11. Risks — What Could Go Wrong

**Risk 1 — Underwhelming yourself.** You've been building this for long enough that you may downplay it. Don't. The clinical safety specification is genuinely impressive — most behavioral health AI products do not have anything like this depth of thinking. Walk in owning it.

**Risk 2 — Vague positivity.** The most common bad outcome of high-value meetings is everyone leaving feeling good with no concrete next step. Force the structural conversation. Push for the specific ask. Don't let the meeting end without a defined next move.

**Risk 3 — Overpitching.** Resist the urge to fill silence. Senior people evaluate you on restraint as much as on substance.

**Risk 4 — Asking the wrong person for the wrong thing.** If you ask Graham for money, you've burned his highest-value asset (network). If you ask Cohen for vague partnership, you've squandered the chance to define structure. Ask each of them for the thing only they can give.

**Risk 5 — Letting the clinical depth go unseen.** If you don't bring the Mode C summary printout and the safety rules one-pager, the meeting becomes about your *story*, not your *substance*. Bring the artifacts.
