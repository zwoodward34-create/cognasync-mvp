To collect and analyze vocal biomarkers without crossing into the regulatory boundary of Software as a Medical Device (SaMD), an AI platform must be carefully designed around its **intended use**, the **types of data it processes**, and **how the information is presented** to healthcare professionals (HCPs).  
Under FDA guidelines, software that performs a medical purpose—such as diagnosing, treating, mitigating, or preventing a disease—is generally regulated as a medical device 1, 2\. Because vocal biomarkers analyze speech patterns (like pitch, speaking rate, or emotional valence) to detect conditions like depression 3, 4, they closely approach this boundary.  
To support psychiatric practices while remaining exempt from SaMD regulations, a platform should navigate the **Clinical Decision Support (CDS) software exemption** or fall under **FDA enforcement discretion** by adhering to the following guardrails:  
**1\. Avoid Interpreting the Clinical Implications of "Signals"**The FDA's revised CDS guidance clearly states that if software is intended to acquire, process, or analyze a continuous "pattern" or "signal" (such as a continuous heart rate monitor reading or an acoustic voice recording) and then **assess or interpret its clinical implications**, the software remains a regulated device 5-7.

* **How to comply:** To avoid this, the AI should avoid outputting automated clinical interpretations derived directly from the raw acoustic signal (e.g., "The patient's vocal jitter indicates severe depression"). Instead, the platform could transcribe the audio to extract discrete "medical information" (such as linguistic content or word choice), which the FDA treats differently than continuous physiological signals 6, 7\.

**2\. Do Not Provide Specific Diagnoses, Directives, or Risk Scores**To qualify for the non-device CDS exemption, the AI cannot provide a specific preventative, diagnostic, or treatment output or direction 8, 9\.

* **How to comply:** The AI platform must be framed as a tool that *informs or enhances* an HCP's decision-making, rather than directing it. It cannot be used in time-critical, urgent-action scenarios where a clinician might suffer from "automation bias" and blindly trust the AI 8\. Furthermore, the FDA explicitly notes that generating a **disease-specific risk score or probability** (e.g., "90% probability of a depressive relapse") fails the CDS exemption and crosses the boundary into SaMD 9\. The system should instead provide prioritized lists of options or highlight longitudinal behavioral trends 9, 10\.

**3\. Ensure Complete Transparency for Independent Review**A critical requirement for the CDS exemption is that the HCP must be able to independently review the basis for the AI's recommendations so they do not rely primarily on the software 8, 11\.

* **How to comply:** The platform must use "Explainable AI" principles. The software interface and labeling must provide plain-language descriptions of the underlying algorithm, the logic and methods relied upon, and the characteristics of the training data used 11\. The output should also present the relevant patient-specific information alongside the AI's insights, allowing the psychiatrist to apply their own clinical judgment to make the final decision 11, 12\.

**4\. Leverage "Low-Risk" Enforcement Discretion**If the platform's functions technically meet the definition of a device, it might still avoid active FDA regulation if it falls under specific enforcement discretion policies for low-risk products.

* **How to comply:** The FDA exercises enforcement discretion for software functions that help diagnosed patients self-manage their disease or maintain behavioral coping skills (e.g., providing an anxiety coping "Skill of the Day"), as long as the software does not provide specific treatment suggestions or operate autonomously 13-15.

**5\. Implement a "Human-in-the-Loop" Framework**To further distance the platform from diagnostic medical devices, it should be designed strictly as a screening or triage assistant for trained professionals. For example, AI platforms analyzing mental health through social media or digital phenotypes are often established as "human-in-the-loop" systems. They flag concerning linguistic patterns or acute risks for human moderation, rather than rendering isolated clinical diagnoses 16, 17\.
