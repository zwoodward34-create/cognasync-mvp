# What type of data and reports would a psychiatrist find most useful for informing care between appointments

A psychiatrist will usually find the most useful between-visit data to be a **short, trend-focused summary** of symptoms, sleep, medication use, side effects, and major stressors, rather than long raw notes or a full journal dump. The goal is to show what changed, how often it changed, and whether the pattern is improving, worsening, or stable.[^1][^2][^3]

## Most useful data

- Daily mood ratings, ideally with a brief note on context, because the number alone is less useful than the trend and what was happening around it.[^1]
- Energy level, especially when it does not match mood, since that mismatch can be clinically informative.[^1]
- Sleep timing, duration, and quality, because consistency and variability matter, not just total hours.[^2][^1]
- Medication adherence, including missed doses, late doses, recent changes, and any reason for skipping.[^2][^1]
- Side effects, such as sedation, agitation, nausea, sexual side effects, or tremor, especially if they affect functioning or adherence.[^2]
- Notable triggers or stressors, like conflict, work deadlines, travel, alcohol use, or major schedule changes.[^1][^2]
- Functioning in daily life, such as work performance, school attendance, social withdrawal, or ability to complete routine tasks.[^3][^2]
- Standardized symptom scores when available, like PHQ-9 or GAD-7, because they make progress easier to compare over time.[^3][^2]


## Best report format

A psychiatrist is most likely to use a **one-page summary** or dashboard that highlights averages, variability, missed medication doses, and flagged events, because that is easier to interpret during a short appointment than raw logs. A good report should answer three questions quickly: what changed, when did it change, and what might have caused it. Charts or simple trend lines are usually more useful than long narrative entries.[^2][^1]

## What to avoid

- Long, unstructured journals with too much detail and no pattern.
- Raw data dumps that make it hard to see trends.
- Vague summaries like “doing okay” without numbers, examples, or dates.
- Only recording bad days, which can make the picture look worse or less balanced than it is.


## Practical template

A useful weekly or biweekly report could include:

- Mood average and range.
- Energy average and range.
- Sleep hours, bedtime/wake-time consistency, and sleep quality.
- Medication adherence and missed doses.
- Side effects.
- Top 1–3 stressors or triggers.
- Any safety concerns or urgent changes.
- A brief note on work, relationships, or daily functioning.

For most psychiatrists, the most valuable thing is not volume of data but **signal**: enough information to guide medication decisions, detect relapse early, and understand whether symptoms are tied to illness, life events, or treatment side effects.
