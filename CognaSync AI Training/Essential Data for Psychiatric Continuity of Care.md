To provide effective ongoing care between visits, a psychiatrist needs specific data to track your progress, ensure your safety, and evaluate how well your treatment plan is working. The most important information includes:

* **Symptom tracking and daily logs:** Data from **mood journals, sleep logs, and symptom-tracking apps** gives your provider real, objective data about how your condition fluctuates daily 1\. This helps the psychiatrist measure symptom reduction, improvements in your daily functioning, and your subjective well-being 2\.  
* **Medication adherence and side effects:** Your provider needs an honest account of your **adherence to the treatment plan**, which includes taking your medication exactly as prescribed 3\. They also need to monitor whether you are experiencing any side effects so they can adjust your dosage or try a different approach to maximize benefits while minimizing adverse reactions 4\.  
* **Laboratory and medical test results:** If you are taking certain psychiatric medications, your psychiatrist will need ongoing **laboratory monitoring to check medication blood levels and prevent physical side effects**. For example, medications like lithium, valproic acid, and clozapine require regular monitoring of therapeutic blood levels 5\. They may also monitor your liver and renal function, or order metabolic parameters and electrocardiograms (ECGs) for patients taking antipsychotics 6\.  
* **Insights from other care providers:** With your consent, **communication with your therapist or primary care doctor** helps your psychiatrist coordinate your care. Insights gained from your talk therapy sessions can provide your psychiatrist with a much clearer picture of your emotional progress and evolving needs 7, 8\.  
* **Worsening symptoms or crisis events:** Your psychiatrist needs to be informed immediately if **things get worse between visits**, or if you had to utilize an after-hours crisis plan, emergency room, or hotline 8\.  
* **Efforts in practicing therapeutic skills:** If your overall treatment plan includes psychotherapy, the psychiatrist will want to know if you are **actively practicing the coping skills and strategies** you are learning in therapy during your daily life 3\.
