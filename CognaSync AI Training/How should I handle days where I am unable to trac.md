# How should I handle days where I am unable to track symptoms

When you can’t track symptoms on a given day, the best approach is to **keep the record intact without breaking the chain**, while being kind to yourself and avoiding guilt. A few practical strategies work well:

### 1. Use a “bare minimum” entry

On tough days, write just **one short line** instead of skipping entirely. Examples:

- “Too fatigued to track”
- “Doing same as yesterday”
- “No tracking today – mood low, bed rest”
- “Forgot to track; roughly okay”[^1][^2]

This prevents unexplained gaps and still signals to your psychiatrist that something made tracking hard.[^2]

### 2. Treat missed days as data

Missing tracking can itself be meaningful information. At your next session, note:

- Which days were missed
- Whether they were extreme low-energy, high-anxiety, or chaotic days
- Any pattern (e.g., missed days often follow poor sleep or medication changes)[^3][^1]


### 3. Use the “good enough” method on hard days

Instead of detailed ratings, use simple categories:

- Mood: worse / same / better
- Energy: low / medium / high
- Functioning: could not work / managed some tasks / normal
- Sleep: poor / okay / good[^1]

You can also use a single 0–10 score if even “worse/same/better” feels like too much.

### 4. Allow planned breaks and resets

It’s okay to:

- Pause tracking during vacations or mental health resets
- Take a break for a day or two when you’re overwhelmed
- Restart without self-judgment[^3][^1]

Skipping a day is not failure; consistency over weeks matters more than perfection on every single day.[^3]

### 5. Be honest and non-judgmental in your notes

Use neutral language instead of self-criticism:

- Instead of “bad day” → “low energy, high anxiety”
- Instead of “I failed” → “couldn’t track today due to fatigue”[^3]

This helps you stay consistent and reduces the emotional burden of tracking itself.[^1][^3]

### 6. If you forget, add a quick catch-up note

If you remember later that you missed a day or two:

- Add a brief entry with the date and a short note:
“May 28–29: missed tracking; roughly same as usual, more tired”[^4]
- Note anything you remember clearly; if details are fuzzy, say so.


### 7. Align tracking with your capacity

On days when energy is low:

- Track at the time of day when you’re most alert (morning or evening)[^3]
- Use a simple 1–2 question version:
    - “How was your day overall? (worse/same/better)”
    - “Could you do your main daily tasks? (yes/no)”[^1]


### 8. Discuss your tracking style with your psychiatrist

It can help to tell your psychiatrist:

- Which days you miss and why
- How many days per month you skip
- Whether tracking feels burdensome or helpful

They can help you adjust what to track so it’s beneficial without becoming overwhelming.[^1]

***

**Simple rule:**
If tracking the full template feels impossible, do the **minimum viable entry**. A one-line note is far better than a blank day and keeps the record useful for your care.
