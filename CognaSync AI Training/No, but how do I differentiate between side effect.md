# No, but how do I differentiate between side effects and symptoms

To differentiate between **side effects** and **symptoms**, focus on **cause** and **timing**:

## Core distinction

- **Side effects** are **unintended changes caused by the medication itself** (starting, stopping, or changing the dose).[^1]
- **Symptoms** are **part of your underlying condition** (e.g., depression, anxiety, bipolar, OCD, PTSD) and reflect the illness, not the medication.[^1]


## Key differences

| Feature | Side Effects | Symptoms (of your condition) |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Cause** | Caused by the medication | Caused by your underlying mental health condition |
| **Timing** | Start after adding, stopping, or changing dose; often within **days to weeks** [^1] | Present **before** starting medication, or return when medication is reduced/missed [^1] |
| **Pattern** | Often **steady** and tied to dosing; may be worse shortly after taking the dose | Often **linked to stressors**, sleep, or life events; may wax and wane |
| **Examples** | Nausea, dry mouth, drowsiness, feeling "flat" or "wired," temporary headache, sexual side effects, emotional numbness [^1][^2] | Persistent sadness, racing thoughts, compulsions, hypervigilance, hopelessness, intrusive thoughts [^1] |
| **Change with dose** | May worsen with higher doses, improve with lower doses | Not directly tied to dose changes; instead tied to illness severity |

## Practical questions to ask yourself

When you notice something new or worse, ask:

1. **Timing:**
    - Did this start **shortly after a medication change** (new med, dose change, time change)? → More likely **side effect**.[^1]
    - Has this been present **for months**, or does it feel like your usual pattern? → More likely **symptom**.[^1]
2. **Is it familiar?**
    - Does it feel like **the same kind of hopelessness, anxiety, or intrusive thoughts** you had before treatment? → Likely a **symptom flare**.[^1]
    - Is it a **new type of feeling** you didn't have before the medication? → More likely a **side effect**.[^1]
3. **Pattern across days:**
    - Is it **steady** and present most days, especially if it correlates with taking the medication? → More likely **side effect**.[^1]
    - Does it **spike with stressors, poor sleep, or substance use**, and improve on calmer days? → More likely **symptom**.[^1]
4. **Physical vs. emotional:**
    - **Physical** changes like nausea, dry mouth, drowsiness, headache, tremor, sexual dysfunction are more often **side effects**.[^2][^1]
    - **Core mood/thought patterns** like persistent sadness, racing thoughts, compulsions, hypervigilance are more often **symptoms**.[^1]

## Examples

- **Side effect:**
    - Feeling more tired than usual for a few days after starting an antidepressant.
    - Mild nausea or headache that starts within a week of a dose increase.
    - Emotional numbness or feeling "flat" after starting a new medication.[^1]
- **Symptom:**
    - Ongoing fatigue with low mood and loss of interest that has been present for months → likely **depression**.[^1]
    - Racing thoughts that return when you miss doses or reduce medication → likely **bipolar** or underlying condition.[^1]
    - The same kind of intrusive thoughts or hopelessness you had before treatment → likely **symptom flare**.[^1]


## When to contact your prescriber

- **Mild or temporary side effects** (e.g., mild nausea, slight drowsiness, temporary headache) often improve as your body adjusts over a few days.[^2][^1]
- **Persistent or bothersome side effects** (e.g., ongoing insomnia, sexual side effects, emotional numbness, increased anxiety that doesn't settle) should be reported to your psychiatrist.[^1]
- **Red flags needing urgent care:**
    - Thoughts of self-harm or suicide
    - Extreme restlessness or agitation
    - Hallucinations or feeling detached from reality in a new way
    - Sudden, severe mood swings or behavior changes
    - Allergic reactions (hives, swelling, trouble breathing)
    - Signs of serotonin syndrome (confusion, fever, stiff muscles, rapid heart rate)[^1]


## Simple rule of thumb

- **New, dose-related, and started after a medication change** → likely **side effect**.
- **Familiar, long-standing, or returning when you miss doses** → likely **symptom of your condition**.

When in doubt, **track what you're feeling** (when it started, how long it lasts, what makes it better/worse) and share it with your psychiatrist. They can help you interpret whether it's a side effect or symptom based on your history and medication changes.[^1]
