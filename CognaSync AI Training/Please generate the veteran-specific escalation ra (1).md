# Please generate the veteran-specific escalation rationale examples

Here are veteran-specific **escalation rationale examples** you can use in a policy or annotation guide. They are written to justify why a case should move up the safety ladder without assuming a diagnosis.

## Veteran-specific rationale examples

### Level 1: Passive concern

- “Veteran mentions feeling detached, sleeping poorly, and ‘not caring much lately,’ but does not express intent or a plan. Veteran status increases concern because transition stress and prior trauma can make passive hopelessness more clinically significant.”
- “Audio shows low energy and reduced speech output compared with baseline. In a veteran with recent reintegration stress, this supports closer monitoring even without explicit self-harm language.”


### Level 2: Elevated concern

- “Veteran reports recurring thoughts that life is not worth it and has withdrawn from family and peers. Because veterans may have elevated risk when isolation and trauma symptoms combine, this warrants prompt human review.”
- “The person describes increased irritability, sleep disruption, and repeated references to firearms in a context of distress. Even without direct intent, veterans-specific access and trauma context justify escalation to safety review.”
- “Speech is more agitated, faster, and harder to interrupt than baseline. In a veteran with possible PTSD-related destabilization, this pattern should be treated as more concerning than the same signal in isolation.”


### Level 3: High risk

- “Veteran expresses wanting to die and acknowledges a possible method, but is vague about timing. Given direct ideation plus military-related access considerations, this should be escalated immediately.”
- “Veteran states they have been thinking about harming themselves, have access to a weapon, and are avoiding contact with others. The combination of intent, means, and isolation requires urgent crisis escalation.”
- “Recent behavioral change, insomnia, and explicit self-harm statements occur after a major life transition. In a veteran, this cluster supports high-risk classification and human intervention.”


### Level 4: Imminent danger

- “Veteran states they may act tonight and identifies a specific method. This is an imminent danger case and must trigger emergency workflow.”
- “Veteran reports current intent, has means nearby, and is unable to agree to safety steps. Immediate emergency response is required.”
- “The person is actively preparing to self-harm and cannot be redirected. Veteran status does not change the action here: this is immediate crisis escalation.”


## Rationale language patterns

When writing veteran-specific rationales, use phrases like:

- “transition stress.”
- “trauma-related destabilization.”
- “isolation after service.”
- “firearm access relevance.”
- “PTSD-related arousal or agitation.”
- “reintegration difficulties.”

These phrases help explain why the case is elevated without overclaiming certainty.

## Safer wording rules

- Say “increases concern” rather than “proves risk.”
- Say “supports escalation” rather than “confirms suicidality.”
- Say “requires human review” rather than “diagnosed.”
- Keep the focus on observable signals plus veteran context.


## Example template

You can reuse this format:

> “In a veteran, [observed signal] plus [contextual factor] increases concern because [reason]. This does not establish diagnosis, but it does justify [level of escalation].”
