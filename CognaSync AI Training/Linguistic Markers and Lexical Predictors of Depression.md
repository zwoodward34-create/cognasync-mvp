Research demonstrates that analyzing the lexical features—the specific words and linguistic patterns—people use in online posts and spontaneous speech is highly effective for screening depression. The most useful linguistic markers include:

* **Negative Emotion Words:** A greater frequency of words expressing negative affect, such as "sad," "lonely," "miserable," "angry," and "hate," is one of the strongest predictors of depression 1-3. In longitudinal studies tracking social media behavior, the use of negative emotion words showed a very high positive correlation (+0.72) with worsening depressive symptoms over time 4\.  
* **First-Person Singular Pronouns:** The extensive use of self-referential words like "I," "me," and "my" is a hallmark linguistic feature of depression 3\. This reflects a high degree of self-attentional focus and inward rumination, which are core cognitive features of the disorder 5-7. This feature also correlates strongly (+0.64) with higher depression severity scores 4\.  
* **Sleep-Related and Somatic Terms:** Because depression heavily impacts physical health and circadian rhythms, words related to sleep disturbances (e.g., "insomnia," "asleep," "night") and physical ailments (e.g., "fatigue," "pain," "chest") are prominent in the discourse of depressed individuals 8, 9\. Sleep-related terms specifically show a significant positive correlation (+0.58) with depressive symptoms 4\.  
* **Reduced Social and Positive Affect Words:** Individuals experiencing depression tend to use fewer words related to positive emotions and social connections 3\. Conversely, the frequent use of "social words" (referencing family, friends, or groups) is negatively correlated with depression (-0.48), meaning that socially engaged language is actually associated with a stabilization or decline in depressive symptoms 4, 10\.  
* **Inhibition and Cognitive Words:** The use of inhibition words (e.g., "avoid," "deny," "safe") is common as individuals express feelings of vulnerability, fright, or avoidance behaviors 11\. When analyzing spoken language, the overall proportion of specific word classes—such as negations, adjectives, verbs, and sentiment tokens—along with overall syntactic complexity, also act as validated psycholinguistic features for screening depression 12\.
