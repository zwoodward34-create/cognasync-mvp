# Warning signs that grief has become complicated grief

Complicated grief (also called **prolonged grief disorder**) occurs when grief remains **intense, debilitating, and doesn't ease over time**, making it hard to recover and resume your life after a loss.[^1][^5]

## Key warning signs

### 1. Duration and intensity

- **Grief lasting too long**:
    - For **adults**: symptoms persisting **at least one year** after the death[^5][^1]
    - For **children/adolescents**: at least **six months**[^7]
    - Sometimes considered after **6 months** if symptoms are severe[^6][^8]
- **Symptoms don't improve** with time[^1][^6]
- Grief **intensifies** as more time passes rather than gradually easing[^4]
- You feel **"stuck"** in grief for many months with no change in intensity[^6]


### 2. Emotional symptoms

| Symptom | Description |
| :-- | :-- |
| **Intense, persistent longing** | Overwhelming yearning or pining for the deceased that doesn't ease [^1][^5][^8] |
| **Persistent sadness/anger** | Nonstop anger, bitterness, guilt, or fear that doesn't allow relief [^3] |
| **Inability to accept death** | Trouble believing the person is gone; may fantasize about them returning [^1][^3][^8] |
| **Emotional numbness** | Feeling shocked, stunned, dazed, or completely numb for many months [^1][^5][^9] |
| **Bitterness about loss** | Feeling anger and resentment toward the world or situation [^1][^4][^5] |
| **Intrusive thoughts** | Constant thoughts about the person or circumstances of death that feel stuck on a loop [^3][^7][^8] |
| **Loss of meaning** | Feeling life has no purpose or meaning without the loved one [^1][^5][^6] |
| **Loneliness** | Feeling intensely lonely even when other people are around [^9] |

### 3. Behavioral and functional changes

| Symptom | Description |
| :-- | :-- |
| **Trouble with routines** | Difficulty carrying out normal daily activities (work, school, chores, self-care) [^1][^5][^6] |
| **Social withdrawal** | Isolating from others, avoiding social activities, withdrawing from daily life [^1][^3][^4][^6] |
| **Avoidance** | Excessively avoiding reminders of the loss (places, people, objects) [^1][^5][^7][^9] |
| **Obsession with reminders** | Extreme focus on mementos, or conversely, strong aversion to all reminders [^1][^4][^5] |
| **Reckless behavior** | Impulsive, self-destructive, or potentially dangerous behavior [^4] |
| **Declining physical health** | Stress-related issues like high blood pressure, headaches, sleep problems, loss of appetite [^7][^10] |
| **Sleep problems** | Insomnia, sleeping at odd hours, difficulty resting [^4][^7] |
| **Neglecting self-care** | Lack of attention to grooming, personal appearance, feeding yourself, shopping [^4][^6] |

### 4. Mood and mental health symptoms

- **Depression, deep sadness, guilt, or self-blame**[^5][^1]
- **Belief you did something wrong** or could have prevented the death[^1][^5]
- **Worsening of preexisting mental health conditions** (depression, PTSD, anxiety, substance abuse)[^4]
- **Irritability and hair-trigger temper** that makes communication difficult[^4]
- **Lack of trust in others** or estrangement from relationships[^8][^1]
- **Inability to enjoy life** or think back on positive experiences with the loved one[^5][^1]


### 5. Suicidal thoughts (urgent warning)

Seek **immediate help** if you experience:

- Feeling that **life isn't worth living** without your loved one[^1][^5]
- **Wishing you had died** along with your loved one[^5][^1]
- **Thoughts of suicide** or actual suicide attempts[^3][^7][^4]
- Escalating longing that becomes **all-consuming**[^3]


## When to seek professional help

Contact your doctor or a mental health professional if you have:

- **Intense grief and problems functioning that don't improve at least one year** after the passing of your loved one[^1]
- **Three or more symptoms** persisting beyond **6 months**[^9]
- Grief that **disrupts your daily life**[^10]
- Feelings that are **consistently sad, lonely, or hopeless** with no adequate support[^10]
- You're **struggling to feed yourself, shop for groceries, or return to work** after several months[^6]


## How complicated grief differs from normal grief

| Normal grief | Complicated grief |
| :-- | :-- |
| Intense pain at first, but **gradually eases** over time [^5] | **Painful emotions don't improve** with time [^1] |
| You can **eventually accept** the loss and move forward [^5] | **Problems accepting** the death persist [^1][^5] |
| You can **resume your life** and daily routines | **Trouble carrying out normal routines** [^1][^5] |
| You can **enjoy life** and fondly remember the person | **Inability to enjoy life** or recall positive experiences [^1][^5] |
| Grief comes in waves, with **periods of relief** | **Constant, unrelenting** grief with no respite [^6] |

## Key takeaway

Complicated grief is when **intense, long-lasting symptoms of grief**, together with **ongoing problems in coping with life**, go on for **more than six months** (or a year for adults) without improvement. If you're still feeling flattened by sadness or completely numb after many months, and struggling with daily functioning, it's a warning sign that you need more help.[^6]

With **professional treatment** (often grief-focused therapy like Complicated Grief Treatment or CBT), even the worst feelings of grief can be resolved and the most disabling symptoms overcome.[^4]
